# Reflection Exercise — Python Basics Part 2 In-Class Assignment

## Purpose

This reflection is meant to help you understand your performance honestly and clearly. Whether your performance was strong, average, or weak, the goal is the same: to identify what your result reveals about your current level, your preparation, your habits, and your next steps.

This is **not** about blaming yourself, protecting your pride, or giving shallow answers. It is about describing reality accurately enough that you can either improve weak areas or continue building on what is already working.

## Duration

50–60 minutes

## Instructions

* Answer **all questions**.
* Write in **full sentences**.
* Most answers should be about **one paragraph**. Some may take **two short paragraphs** if needed.
* As a guideline, aim for **at least 80 words per question**.
* Be honest, concrete, and specific.
* Do not give vague answers such as “I will try harder” or “I need to improve everything.”
* Treat this as a serious self-assessment. The point is not to protect your pride. The point is to understand reality well enough to act on it.

---

## Questions

### 1. How do you evaluate your performance in this assignment?

Do you think your performance was strong, acceptable, weak, or somewhere in between? Explain why. Base your answer on the quality of your actual work, not only on your feelings during the assignment.

### 2. What parts of the assignment did you handle well, and what parts were more difficult for you?

Be specific. You may mention things such as function signatures, type hints, return values, dictionaries, classes, nested loops, matrices, reading instructions carefully, or managing your time.

### 3. Compared to your off-class programming work, such as the 60 Fundamental Exercises or other take-home work, was there a discrepancy in your performance?

If yes, explain what you think caused the difference. If no, explain what that consistency suggests about your preparation and current ability.

### 4. What did this assignment reveal about your current programming level?

Describe both your strengths and your limits. What can you do independently with reasonable confidence? What can you not yet do reliably on your own?

### 5. Which issues in your work came more from lack of understanding, and which came more from lack of attention to detail?

Use at least **two concrete examples**. If you performed well overall, you may also reflect on the kinds of mistakes you avoided and why you think you were able to avoid them.

### 6. How did you prepare for the Python Basics Part-2 assignment, and how effective was that preparation?

What did you do that helped? What did you fail to do, or what could you have done better? Answer honestly and specifically.

### 7. When you became uncertain, stuck, or under pressure during the assignment, how did you respond?

If you struggled, explain how you reacted and whether that response helped or hurt you. If you remained relatively calm and effective, explain what helped you stay focused and continue working.

### 8. What should your next step be from here?

If you did poorly, what exactly do you need to rebuild? If you did reasonably well or very well, what should you continue doing, and what should you still strengthen? Give a concrete plan for the next **2–3 weeks**.

A strong answer should describe real actions, real structure, and real responsibility — not just good intentions.

## Final Note

A good result should teach you something. A disappointing result should also teach you something. In both cases, the value of this reflection comes from being honest enough to describe where you are now and disciplined enough to decide what to do next.

The truth can be uncomfortable, but it is useful. If you can face your current level honestly, then you can improve it. If you hide from it, excuse it, or leave it vague, you will likely repeat the same pattern. Use this reflection to describe your situation clearly enough that your next step becomes obvious.

This exercise should also help you practice reasoning more clearly about yourself, your choices, and your learning. That habit will help you not only in future courses, but also later in your professional career, where your ability to reflect, adapt, and respond to reality matters a great deal.
